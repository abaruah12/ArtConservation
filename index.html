Problem

Art conservators assess and restore defects on hundreds of paintings every year at museums and art galleries. Defect analysis is conducted when artworks travel between galleries every year for exhibitions. The National Gallery of Canada estimates that a total of 800 to 1000 artworks are exchanged via a loan program every year. During the transit phase, damage can occur across any layer of the painting, with cracking being the most common deteriorations. There is a need for a faster, easy to use and accurate mechanism for detection of cracks in paintings.

Analysis Time

Current analysis times range from 30 min to 4 hours per art piece. Goal is to minimize both survey and analysis times.

Crack detection

Current analysis techniques are capable of detecting cracks visible to the human eye. At the very least crack widths of 10μm should be detected.

Costs

iPads are currently primarily used by conservators to survey and analyse a painting for defects. The total product costs must therefore be approximately equivalent to the cost of an iPad Pro.

Current analysis methods are time intensive and require well experienced analysts!

Solution

This project aims to deliver a solution for automating the survey and analysis process of defect detection in paintings, saving a museum thousands of hours in analysis time a year. Automatic crack detection is the specific goal of this project, to be performed with the usage of consumer grade DSLR mounted on a gantry system. The capture of the images and detection of defects is conducted in real time.

Core Components

  • Software: Crack Detection

    Key function is for the detection of cracks of minimum width 10μm. Processing of the images captured by the camera is conducted on a device (ex. laptop) separate from the scanning apparatus. A defect map of all existing cracks should be produced for the entire painting for the conservator to view and assess.

  • Sensors

    Key function is to capture a set of images of the painting with 10 um details using the camera and flash ring of LED lights. These images are then offloaded to a server where they are stored and available for processing in real time or in a later period by the detection software.

  • Mechanical structure

    Key functions include securing the sensor, removal of ambient light, securing the painting and vibration isolation within the system and from the environment. Support the movement of the camera along the major and minor axes.

  • Motors and Power Management

    Key functions include provision of minimum major and minor axes resolution of ±1 mm by the motors and provision of two major voltage rails, 24V and 5V, for the motors, linear actuators, Raspberry Pi and Arduinos.

Weekly Blog: January 30, 2019

Construction of mechanical structure.

The first component of the system built is the mechanical structure. The structure consists of 15 aluminium 80/20 T slots forming a rectangular prism of 90 cm x 75 cm x 50 cm. Components mounted to the structure include the motors and camera lens apparatus. The camera moves along the x and y direction using a pulley system composed of timing belts and bearings. There are a total of 3 motors controlling the movement of the camera in the major and minor axes with 2 on either end of the major axis and one motor on the starting end of the minor axis. Cantilever limit switches on either ends of the major and minor axes help to control the restricted motion of the camera. 3D printerd sliders help to guide and move the rails along both axes. First the rails were bolted in using L brackets to build the skeleton of the structure. Next the motor mounts and the pulley system were attached. Lastly the camera mount was attached onto a slider with the camera being held to the mount with a screw in bolt. For the constraint of lighting control, a blackout cloth is draped over the entire structure to limit the entrance of external lighting into the system. Some issues faced in the assembly were alignment of the rails in the major and minor axes as the camera was moved. This issue was resolved by realigning the entire structure to ensure the rails were parallel and the pulley mounts were properly tightened and secure. The initial design consisted of acrylic mounts for the motors, however these mounts proved to be uncapable of handling the motor weight and thus were replaced by aluminum mounts plates instead, thus strenghtening the motor mounts.

Weekly Blog: February 12, 2019

Construction of electrical structure.

The electrical assembly is composed of the Arduino, Raspberry Pi, motors, motor controllers, power supply, router, and the associated wiring of all components together. The electrical system was integrated onto an acrylic board which was attached to the top corner of the mechanical structure. The motors and limit switches were mounted in their respective locations on the major and minor axes, and twisted pair cables were used to connect the power and signal wires to the main electrical assembly.

Weekly Blog: February 20, 2019

Development of the communication software architecture.

The communication system consists of an Arduino, Raspberry Pi, router, and a laptop. As can be seen in the diagram above, the laptop communicated job parameter and instructions to the Raspberry Pi which subsequently channels the movement instructions to the Arduino for the control of the motors. The Arduino also implements the limit switch logic to protect the camera from crashing into the rails. The Raspberry Pi controls the capture of the image and the flashing of the LED flash ring appended to the camera. After the capture of the images, they are stored locally on the laptop and exposed on a FTP server also. Once an image has been captured, a notification is sent to retrieve it and process the image for defects.

Weekly Blog: February 28, 2019

Development of the defect detection system

The processing of the defects is offloaded to a laptop where the detection module is running. The inputs to this algorithm are the painting size and path of the working directory on the laptop where the processed images are to be stored. The module for defect detection runs via the command line with the specified job parameters, as listed above. A new job is started every time a painting is scanned. After the camera has captured the image for a section of the painting, the detection module retrives the image from the server and stores it locally on the laptop to be processed. The image is processed through Gaussian blurring and morphological operations to aid in the detection of crack like features. Afterwards an edge detection and line transform function are used to retrive the points of the cracks on the painting. These points are then plotted in red on the image, as can be seen in the image above. As each image is processed, the original and processed images are shown side by side to the user so they can be monitored in real time. Images are stitched by the row as they are processed and finally all the rows are stitched together to form the complete defect map of the painting. Conservators can then view this image, or any of the single images, to asses the defect detection in detail. Issues faced in the development of the algorithm include determination of kernel size to use for the blurring and morphological operations and elimination of noise. For the kernel size, numerous types and sizes of kernels were tested till a final one suited for detecting dark cracks in light areas and light cracks in dark areas was found. For the elimination of noise, only cracks which surpassed a certain length were plotted and increased Gaussian blur helped to further smooth the image out.
